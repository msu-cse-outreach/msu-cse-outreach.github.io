Week 5

Very Able Variables

When things change, so do we! As humans, we adapt to our surroundings—consciously and unconsciously—to stay healthy and accomplish our goals. When it's hot, we sweat to cool off; when it's cold, we shiver to warm up; when we're faced with a challenge or a deadline, we narrow our focus to get things done. You might say we're very able to adapt to variable conditions—or, you might cringe at the horrible rhyme—in either case, you can't deny adaptation is crucial in our lives.

Perhaps unsurprisingly, adaptation is crucial in code, too—without adaptation, any computer program will eventually run into a dead-end and crash. Thankfully, variables allow computer programs to adapt to changing circumstances—as their name suggests, they are able to vary when instructed to do so!

Week 4

Fun with Functions

We're surrounded by black boxes—tools that just work when used to complete a task. To most of us, cars are black boxes getting us from point A to point B; TVs are black boxes streaming video and audio into our living room; and phones are black boxes connecting us to our friends and family through social media, texts and calls. Black boxes make our world better—they make complex tasks easy and empower us to accomplish more with less time and energy. It's impossible to imagine modern life without them!

In computer science, these black boxes take the form of functions—structures that hide the details of a complex task behind a simple statement. Functions just work, allowing us to think about other, higher-level concepts when writing code. This week, our coders learned how to write and call their own functions in Python—though you might just say they had fun with functions!

Following a brief review of the commands covered in Weeks 1-3—including color, pensize, begin_fill, end_fill, forward, backward, left, right, and circle—we dove into the fun world of functions. Functions allow us to simplify our code by serving as black boxes: they hide the complexities of a task behind a single statement and allow us to think about higher-level concepts when writing code. In a sense, functions teach Tracy the Turtle new tricks!


Functions are a key foundation in programming—ask your student why!

More concretely, our students learned how to move the three lines of code required to draw a square—namely, (i) a line to loop four times, (ii) a line to move forward, and (iii) a line to turn left—from their main program into a custom-written function. Then, they replaced these three lines in their main program with a single line calling the custom-written function! The function became a black box that just worked and drew a square, allowing our students to think on higher-levels about creating more complex shapes composed of squares.

After introducing functions, we solidified our understanding of their use by working through an example in which Tracy is tasked with drawing a set of colored shapes. By breaking the problem down and defining four separate functions to draw (i) a red diamond, (ii) a blue circle, (iii) a yellow semicircle, and (iv) a green pentagon, we hid the complexities of each shape in four separate black boxes and used those black boxes to produce our final drawing. Pretty nifty, eh?


Functions help us create more complex drawings—ask your student why!

Meeting Recap

Following a lively sli.do icebreaker, students and mentors kicked off Saturday's lesson with an uplifting spotlight video from Khan Academy. Introducing the definition of a growth mindset and how it differs from a fixed mindset, the spotlight underscored the importance of bringing a growth mindset to Technovation every week. Learning to code is difficult, but the challenges we overcome as we navigate this learning process will ultimately make us stronger—we simply need to change the way we see failure! If we view it as an opportunity for improvement instead of a setback, we'll go far—if we're feeling down, we simply need to turn our frown upside-down and remember that all experiences are learning experiences!


Bringing a growth mindset to Technovation each week is key! Ask your student what a growth mindset means to them, and how they can apply it elsewhere in their life.

Next, we jumped into our coding lesson for the week. We reviewed the coordinate plane in which our coding buddy Tracy the Turtle lives, then learned how to turn Tracy left and right. Combining these turns with the command we learned last week which instructs Tracy to move forward, we were able to write a program telling Tracy to draw a square!


Once students learned how to tell Tracy to turn left and right, they were able to instruct Tracy to draw a square!

We followed this exercise with an introduction to loops, the programming structure we mentioned earlier which tells a computer to repeat a set of instructions over, and over, and over, and over, ..., and over. As students learned, loops bring two major advantages: they shorten our code, and make it easier to alter our code later on!


Going in circles isn't always a bad thing... that's exactly what loops are designed to do!

Finally, we learned how to use the setposition and speed commands to fine-tune Tracy's behavior: setposition allows Tracy to jump around across our screen, while speed allows Tracy to move faster or slower! Ask your student to show you these commands in action—they're pretty cool!


With the setposition command, Tracy's only a hop, skip, and a jump away from anywhere on the screen... and with the speed command, she can get there in a flash!

As usual, students concluded Saturday's meeting with a brief reflection and discussion around what went well, what didn't go so well, and what excites them about the week ahead. We hope they're as excited as we are for Week 3!
